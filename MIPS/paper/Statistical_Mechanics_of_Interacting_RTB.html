Statistical Mechanics of Interacting Run-and-Tumble Bacteria

Paper Info.

  • Author: J. Tailleur and M.E. Cates
  • Journal: Phys. Rev. Lett. 100, 218103
  • URL: https://journals.aps.org/prl/abstract/10.1103/PhysRevLett.100.218103
  • Topic: Equilibrium in Active Systems, Run-and-TUmble Bacteria

First Reading

At first, the author mentions that species of bacteria, such as E-coli, experiences a motion with constant speed \(v\) in an almost straight line trajectory. This motion will be punctuated by a sudden and randomizations in directions. Such process is called tumbling. Because of its active motion with randomizations, it is not difficult to deduce that, macroscopically,the process will experience a diffusive like motion. However, such process must have a larger diffusivity than the thermal diffusion due to its motility. Therefore, we always ignor the true Brownian motion when discussing such run-and-tumble system. An one dimensional case is discussed by the author. Let's define \(R(x, t)\) , \(L(x, t)\) are the probabilirty to find a single run-and-tumble particle at position \(x\) and time \(t\). Assume they moe with velocity \(v_{R, L}\), respectively, and changing dirction from \(R\) to \(L\) with probability \(\alpha_R\), and vice versa. The master equations of these two probabilities are \[ \begin{align*} \dot{R} &= -\nabla(v_R R) - \alpha_R R/2 + \alpha_L L/2 \\ \dot{L} &= -\nabla(v_L L) + \alpha_R R/2 - \alpha_L L/2. \end{align*} \] If we define the total probability \(p\equiv R+L\), the corresponding time derivative should be \(\dot{p} = -\nabla (v_R R-v_L L) \equiv -\nabla J \) To examine the diffusive limit, we take the second time derivate of \(p\) to zero and rearrange the formula, we can find that (though I still can not understand why we need to take the second time derivative to zero and how should we rearrange it) \[ (1+\xi_1) J + \xi_2 \nabla J = \mathcal{J} \] \[ \mathcal{J} = - \mathcal{D} \nabla p +\mathcal{V}p \] \[ \mathcal{D} = v_Rv_L /\alpha \] \[ \mathcal{V} = (\alpha_L v_R - \alpha_R v_L)/2\alpha - v\nabla(v_Rv_L/v)/\alpha \] \[ \xi_1 = [v_R\nabla(v_R/v) - v_L\nabla(v_L/v)]/2\alpha \] \[ \xi_2 = (v_R-v_L)/\alpha \] If we assume that the spatial derivative of all quantities are in the order of a small quantity \(\epsilon\), we can neglect \(\nabla J\) and find that for first order \[ \dot{p} = - \nabla J, \qquad J= -D \nabla p - Vp, \] with \(D \equiv \mathcal{D}/(1+\xi_1)\) and \(V \equiv \mathcal{V}/(1+\xi_1)\). As we know, a diffusion equation can be derived by Langevin equation. Thus, a Langevin equation that has the previous derived flux should be \[ \dot{x}_i = A(x_i) + C(x_i) L_i(t), \] where \(L_i(t)\) is a unit-varience Gaussian white noise, \(C^2 = 2D\) and \(A =V + \partial D/\partial x_i\). This equation shows the behavior of \(i\)th particle in the system. However, we only care about the macroscopic quantities. Therefore, we corase-grain the system by defining \(\rho(x) = \sum_i \rho_i(x) = \sum_i \delta(x-x_i) \). Therefore, a macroscopic version of the time derivative physical quantity \(f(x_i)\) according to Ito-Langecin equations is \[ \begin{align*} \dot{f}(x_i) &= (A+C L_i)\partial f/\partial x_i + (C^2/2)\partial^2 f/\partial x_i^2 \\ &= \int \rho_i(x,t)[(A+CL_i)f' +D f'']dx. \end{align*} \] Since \(\dot{f}(x_i) = \int \dot{\rho}_if(x)dx\), we find that \[ \dot{\rho}_i = -\nabla(A\rho_i) + \nabla^2(D\rho) - \nabla(L_i C \rho_i). \] Sum over all particles shows that \[ \dot{\rho} = -\nabla(A\rho) + \nabla^2(D\rho) - \sum_i \nabla(L_i C \rho_i) \equiv \nabla J_C \] Thus, \[ J = \rho\{V+\nabla[\delta/\delta\rho] D\} - D\nabla \rho + \sqrt{2D\rho} \Lambda, \] where \(\langle \Lambda(x, t) \Lambda(x', t') \rangle = \delta(t-t')\delta(x-x')\) . Though I still understand how to derive, the final Fokker-Plank for many body probability \(\mathcal{P}\) is \[ \dot{\mathcal{P}} = \int dx \frac{\delta}{\delta \rho(x)} \nabla[\rho V - D \nabla \rho - D\rho(\nabla \frac{\delta}{\delta \rho(x)})] \mathcal{P} \] It seems that the author tend to use an effective free energy, which is analogy to statistical mechanics, to find the property of run-and-tumble system. As we know, the probability distribution when the system reaches its steady state can be described as \(\mathcal{P} \propto e^{-\mathcal{F}[\rho]}\) with \( \mathcal{F} = \mathcal{F}_{ex} + \int \rho(\ln \rho -1)dx\). I am not pretty sure it is Gibbs free energy or not, while it should be notice that it is not neccessary to be Gibbs free energy since the probability distribution must be proportional to the exponential of some energy functions. The result shows that the required condition for using an energy like function to describe is \[ \frac{V([\rho], x)}{D([\rho, x])} = -\nabla \left[\frac{\delta \mathcal{F}_{ex}[\rho]}{\delta \rho(x)}\right]. \] If we consider the symmetry influence of the free energy, with even parity, \(\rho(x)\leftrightarrow \rho(-x) \), and \(\alpha_{L}(x) v_L(x) \leftrightarrow \alpha_{R}(x) v_R(x)\). Then, \[ \nabla \left[\frac{\delta \mathcal{F}_{ex}[\rho]}{\delta \rho(x)}\right] = \Psi + \nabla \left[\ln\left(\frac{v_Rv_L}{v}\right)\right], \] with \[ \Psi = \frac{\alpha_Rv_L - \alpha_L v_R}{2v_Rv_l}. \] One can find that \(\Psi\) is an antisymmetrical quantity. Thus, to the first order approximation, it can be illustrated by a sigle derivative function. That is, \[ \Psi = \nabla \psi(\rho). \] Similarly, \[ \nabla \left[\ln \frac{v_R v_L}{v}\right] = \nabla \left[\ln v\right] \] Thus, \(\mathcal{F}\simeq \int f(\rho) dx = \int [\rho(\ln \rho -1) + f_{ex}(\rho)]dx\) with \[ f_{ex}(\rho) = \int_0^{\rho} [\psi(u) + ln v(u)] du. \] A corresponding brownian motion can be described as setting \(k_BT=1\), with mobility metrix \(D_{ij}=\delta_{ij}v^2(\rho(x_i))/\alpha(\rho(x_i))\). Thus, according to mean field theory, the system will be unstable when \[ \frac{d^2f}{d\rho^2} = \frac{1}{\rho} + \frac{d}{d\rho}[\psi(\rho)+\ln v(\rho)] \leq 0. \] A special case when the can be understood as \(\psi = 0\). As this happens, instability occurs when\(dv/d\rho \leq -v/\rho \), which means a system will be unstable when the particles inside decrease their velocity so intense that a cluster will form. This can also be satisfies when the tumble duration increases. Thus, this implies that the necessary condition for generating phase separation is that the velocity should be the function of density. Finally, the author test the translational symmetry. One can find that the transilational symmetry will break the relation since we can not consider the system in the diffusive dominating state. That is, \(v_{R, L} = v \pm v_T\), with \(v_T - \mu_T\nabla[\delta H_{int}/\delta \rho(x)]\). Since the final result of \(\mathcal{V}/\mathcal{D}\) can not be written as a functional integral, the statistical mapping is invalid.

Summary

In sum, the author ubuild up a run-and-tumble system. By examing the flux at diffusive limit, the author rewrite the equation to a Lnagevin equation. By corse-grainin the system, the author find the corresponding diffusion equation and the corresponding Fokker-Plank equaion for a many body system. With this, an analogy can be exmained by finding the corresponding free energy. The results show that when the system is even parity and the left-right symmetry, the homogeneous state will be unstable when the desity dependent velocity decelerate too intensive when it confront a dense region. Finally, the author check that the analogy will invalid when the flux is high enough such that the diffusive limit can not be obtained.

Evergreen Notes

The author find the statistical mapping of active system when some conditions are satisfied. The results implied that the phase transition can be obtained when the velocity is the function of density, and the declerating process is intense enough to generate a cluster. These reslut might also imply a detail balance happened in some active systems.
