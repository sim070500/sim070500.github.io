Fluctuation-Dissipation Theory

  1. Home
  2. Fluctuation Dissipation Theory
Fluctuation-dissipation theorem is one crucial theorem for relating the response of a system by an external force and the fluctuation induced by random force. This theorem can be applied to irreversible process. Moreover, it might shed light on a possible analysis of inequilibrium systems.

What is Fluctiation-Dissipation Theorem?

Let's consider a system of brownian particles immersed in in a sloution. The solute and solvent particles collide with these brownian particles. Such motion will induce the so-called Brownian Motion. In contrast, if the brownain particles are charged, an external field will drift these particles to move. In this case, surrounding collisions from solute and solvent particles will generate drag force due to relative motion. This implies that random motion and systemmatically frictional part come from the same origin.
Therefore, fluctuation-dissipation theorem is build up for finding the relationship between response to external disturbance and interal fluctuation in the absense of the disturbance. In general, we use admittance and impedence to quantify response. On the other hand, we use correlation and the corresponding spectum to quantify fluctuation. Finally, our goal is finding fluctuation by response, and vice versa.

Einstein Relation

First of all, we consider a case that brownian particles are driven by external field gradient \( \nabla V \). As we discussed in previous, solvent and solute particles provide drag force in such condition. Thus, for an overdamping system, this implies the force balance equation \[ F = -\nabla V - m\gamma u_d = 0, \] where \(\gamma \) is the drag force provided by surrounding particles and \(u_d\) is the drift velocity of brownian particles. Thus, \(u_d = -\nabla V/ m \gamma \). As the system reaches its steady state, the total flux is balanced between nutrient gradient and velocity flux. That is, \[ J = -D \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} + u_d \phi = 0, \] where \(D\) is the diffusion coefficient. Assuming that the distribution obeys Boltzmann distribution when the system reaches it equilibrium, \(\phi \propto \exp(-V/k_BT) \). Thus, \[ \frac{D\nabla V}{K_B T} \phi + \vec{u_d} \phi = 0, \] and substituting the drift velcoty derived before, we get the relationship between diffusion coefficient and the drag force \(D = k_B T/m\gamma \). This relation correlates two different macroscopic quantities. Since we care about the relationship between these two quantities and the microscopic fluctuations, remind that the root mean square of a random walk equals to \(\sqrt{2Dt}\). Therefore, we can use \[ D = \lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{1}{2t}\langle [x(t) - x(0)]^2 \rangle \] to approximate the diffusion coefficient. \[ \begin{align*} &\because x(t) - x(0) = \int_0^t u(t')dt'\\ &\therefore D = \lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{1}{2t} \int_0^t \int_0^t \langle u(t_1) u(t_2) \rangle dt_1 dt_2 \\ \end{align*} \] Because of the symmetry, we can calculate it by \[ \begin{align*} D &= \lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{1}{2t} \int_0^t \int_0^t \langle u(t_1) u(t_2) \rangle dt_1 dt_2 \\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{1}{t} \int_0^t dt_1 \int_0^{t-t_1} \langle u(t_1) u(t +t') \rangle dt' \end{align*} \] Since if \(F(t_1) \equiv \int_0^{t-t_1} \langle u(t_1) u(t +t')\rangle dt' \) is continuous and finite, there exist a finite value \(u_0\) such that \[ \int_0^t dt_1 F_{t_1} = F(t_0) (t-0). \] \[ \begin{align*} \Rightarrow D &= \lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{1}{t} \int_0^t F(t_1) dt_1 \\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty} F(t_0) \\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty} \int_0^{t-t_0} \langle u(t_0) u(t_0+t') \rangle dt' \\ &= \int_0^{\infty} \langle u(t_0) u(t_0+t) \rangle dt \end{align*} \] Therefore, the Einstein relation shows that \[ \mu \equiv \frac{1}{m\gamma} = \frac{D}{k_BT} = \frac{1}{k_B T }\int_0^{\infty} \langle u(t_0) u(t_0+t) \rangle dt \] This equation links the mobility(friction coefficient) and the response(\(u_d\)).
