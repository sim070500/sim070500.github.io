The introduction of individual difference

How individual difference infulence the onset of jamming transitions?

In the previous discussion, we assumed that all drivers are identical for simplicity. However, it is impossible due to humanity. For instance, an experienced driver might stop smoother and slower than a novice driver does under the same inter-vehicle distance. Therefore, let's modify the classic Bando's model to introduce such individual difference in the perception of spatial distance. Rminding that the optimal velocity $v_{\text{opt}}(\Delta x)$in Bando's model describes how drivers change their velocity when inter-vehicle distance changes. Thus, when we contral the changing rate of optimal velocities for all drivers at different rate, they behave differently even when the inter-vehicle distances are the same. That is, $$ v_{\text{opt}} = v_{\text{opt}}(w_i\cdot \Delta x_i), $$ where $\Delta x_i$ is inter-vehicle distance between vehile $i$ and $i+1$, and $w_i$ illustrate the behavioral property of $i$-th driver. Fig. 1 shows the profile of optimal velocity for different w. Apparantly, for a small value of w, the optimal velocity changes more smoothly, which illustrate the behavior of an experienced driver. While for a large value of w, the optimal velocity changes more aggressively, which is similar to the behavior of novie driver.

In our study, we assume the set of drivers properties ${w_i}$ forms a Gaussian distribution since it is a comman assumption to illustrate humanity and such assumption will simplyfy our calculations. Though the assumption has been applied, the physical intepretation of the final results imply that the introduction of individual difference will suppress the jamming transition, which is independent of the distribution style. Fig 2 shows one example of the Gassian distribution in our simulations.

With these modification, by normalizing the mean value of the distributions to a unit $\bar{w}=1$, we can introduce the individual difference into our simulations. Since the properties of drivers is a Gassian distribution recently, one hundred realization are carried out for all simulations with different standard deviations of the distribution $\sigma$. Final results shown in Fig. 3 represent phases when the standard deviations and reaction time of drivers are changed. To understand this figure, we can focus on a fixed standard deviation. As we discussed before, for drivers with large reaction time, drivers have no time to react to the inter-vehicle change, and the system start to jam togather. Thus, the bottom region of the figure, which is shown in green squres, represent the jam state of the system. In contrast, drivers with short reaction time can react quick enough to the inter-vehicle change, and the system will remain in steady flow. Therefore, the upper region of the figure, which is shown in blue triangles, represent the steady flow state. Now, if we fix the reaction time and increase the standard deviation, we can find that the system transform from a jamming state into a steady flow state. Apparantly, the introduction of individual difference suppresses the jamming transition.

The simplest way to uncover the physics of suc individual difference induced jamming suppression is by calculating the average inter-vehicle distance change when there are only two vehicles. The equilibrium state will occur under the condition $$ w_1 \Delta x_1 = w_2 \Delta x_2 \\ \Delta x_1 + \Delta x_2 = L \\ $$ Thus, $$ w_1 \Delta x_1 = \frac{w_1w_2}{w_1 + w_2}L. $$ By assuming a symmetric condition $w_1 = \bar{w}+\Delta w$ and $w_2 = \bar{w}-\Delta w$, we get $$ w_1 \Delta x_1 = \frac{\bar{w}^2 - \Delta^2}{2N\bar{w}}L < \frac{\bar{w}}{2N}. $$ This implies that the average inter-vehicle perspective distance is shorter than the condition when all drivers are the same. Since drivers drive less aggressively in high density region (the side in hyperbolic tengent diagram near the origin), drivers have enough time to react the inter-vehicle distance change. Hence, when the individual difference is introduced to the system in high density region, the jamming transition is suppressed.

The derivation of analytical phase boundary solution

In this section, I will brief show you how I derive for analytical solutions of phase boundary. The linearize equation of the perturbation to the steady state in Fourier mode is $$ \ddot{\tilde{y}}_k + \frac{1}{\tau}\dot{\tilde{y}}_k = \frac{f_1}{\tau}\sum_l \tilde{y}_l \tilde{w}_{k-l}[\exp (i\alpha_l)-1]. $$ For the case of classic Bando's model, all drievrs are identical, and the only non-zero fourier coefficeint of $\tilde{w}_i$ is $\tilde{w}_0$. Therefore, all fourier modes of perturbations are decouple in this case. However, since drivers are different in our research, different fourier modes of perturbations couple with each other, which turns the solution not trivial. Since the distribution of drivers properties are a Gaussian distribution, the fourier modes of Gassian random field has been proven as $$ \langle \tilde{w}_0 \rangle= \bar{w}, \quad \langle |\tilde{w}_{k\neq 0}|^2\rangle = \frac{\sigma^2}{N}. $$ Thus, by rewriting the summation into two different part and using matrix form, the equation becomes $$ \frac{d^2}{dt^2}\tilde{\textbf{y}} + \frac{1}{\tau}\frac{d}{dt}\tilde{\textbf{y}} = \frac{f_1}{\tau}(H^0+H^1)\tilde{\textbf{y}}, $$ where $\tilde{\textbf{y}} = (\tilde{y}_{-N/2+1},\tilde{y}_{-N/2+2},\dots, \tilde{y}_{N/2})$, and $$ H^0_{kl} = \tilde{w}_0[\exp (i\alpha_l)-1]\delta_{kl} \\[1.5em] H^1_{kl} = \tilde{w}_{k-l}[\exp (i\alpha_l)-1](1-\delta_{kl}) $$ The first matrix $H^0$ is a diagonal matrix, which represents the conditions when all vehicles are identical. The second matrix $H^1$ is the off-diagonal matrix, which represents the influence of the introduction of individual drivers. Since for a Gaussian distribution, $\langle |\tilde{w}_{k\neq 0}|^2\rangle = \frac{\sigma^2}{N}$, which tends to infinitesimal value when the number of drivers tends to infinite. Thus, we can view the second matrix as a perturbation of the first matrix. Because the first matrix is diagonal and the second matrix is off-diagonal, the first order correction $\langle \phi_1|H^1|\phi_1\rangle = 0$, where $\phi_1$ represent the eigenfucntion of first matrix with the largest eigenvalue. To obtain the influence of the individual differnce, the second-order correction $\lambda^{(2)}_1$ must be calculated. That is $$ \lambda^{(2)}_1 = \sum_{k\neq 1}\frac{|\tilde{w}_{1-k}|^2(e^{i\alpha_k}-1)(e^{i\alpha_1}-1)}{\tilde{w}_0(e^{i\alpha_1}-e^{i\alpha_k})} \simeq - \frac{i\sigma^2}{\tilde{w}_0}\sin \alpha_1. $$ Finally, upto the second-order correction, the equation of motion of the perturbation in fourier mode will turn out to be $$ \ddot{\tilde{y}}_k + \frac{1}{\tau}\dot{\tilde{y}}_k = \frac{f_1}{\tau}\left[ e^{i\alpha_k} -1 - i\frac{\sigma^2}{\tilde{w}_0^2}\sin \alpha_1 \right], $$ and the instability condition is modified accordingly $$ f_1 > \frac{1}{2\tau \tilde{w}_0(1-\sigma^2/\tilde{w}_0^2)^2\cos^2(\alpha_k/2)}. $$ For thermodynamic limit $N\gg 0$ with a fixed density, $\cos^2(\alpha_k/2)\simeq 1$, the phase boundary will be predict when the left hand side equals to the right hand side, and is plotted as the black solid line in Fig. 3.
